## Cotton

Our literature review helped uncover the following insights about cotton fabric:

1. General Characterisitics of Cotton:
-	Softness: Cotton fibers are naturally soft. While this isn't a purely visual identifier, it can be a clue. Softness in fabrics often implies that the fibers can be easily deformed, suggesting loose interactions between polymer chains at the molecular level.
- Matte Luster: While cotton fibers naturally have a high luster, untreated cotton fabrics often exhibit a matte or slightly off-white appearance. This can help distinguish them from more lustrous fabrics like silk or satin. However, it's important to note that treatments like mercerization can significantly enhance the luster of cotton, giving it a sheen.
- Wrinkling: Cotton is known for its tendency to wrinkle easily, especially compared to synthetic fabrics like polyester. Observing how easily a fabric wrinkles can be a visual clue.
- Pilling: Over time, cotton fabrics are prone to pilling, where small balls of fiber form on the surface due to friction. This pilling can dull the luster of the fabric and make it appear slightly fuzzy.

2. Common Weave Patterns
- Plain Weave: A simple, criss-cross pattern that creates a smooth surface, often seen in fabrics like broadcloth, muslin, and calico.
- Twill Weave: A diagonal pattern that is durable and drapes well, commonly used for denim and chino.
- Sateen Weave: This weave creates a smooth and lustrous surface, often used for bed sheets and luxurious garments.
- Other Weaves: The sources describe many other weaves like basketweave, herringbone, houndstooth, and jacquard, each with distinct visual characteristics.

## Corduroy

Our literature review helped uncover the following key visual identifiers of corduroy fabric:

1.	**Distinct Raised Cords or Wales**: The most prominent visual identifier of corduroy is its textured surface with raised cords or wales running parallel to each other. These wales are created by weaving extra sets of fibers into the base fabric. The wales are cut to create a pile, giving corduroy its signature velvety texture.

2.	Wale Width and Count: The width of the wales varies, and this variation is a key factor in distinguishing different types of corduroy

3.	Wale count refers to the number of wales per inch. A lower wale count indicates thicker wales, while a higher wale count signifies thinner wales. Standard corduroy typically has 11-12 wales per inch. Pinwale or needlecord has a high wale count, resulting in very fine ribs. Elephant cord, on the other hand, has a very low wale count (1.5 to 6), giving it a distinctive appearance resembling the folds in an elephant's skin.

4.	Channel Between Wales: Some corduroy fabrics exhibit a channel or a bare area between the wales, where the base fabric is visible. This channel can be more or less pronounced depending on the specific type of corduroy.

5.	Overall Texture: Corduroy has a distinctive velvety feel due to the cut pile. The texture can range from soft and plush to slightly stiff, depending on factors like the fiber content, wale count, and finishing processes.

6.	Color and Dyeing: Corduroy is available in a wide range of colors. One unique dyeing technique, pigment dyeing, results in an irregular, faded pattern that becomes more pronounced with washing. This mottled appearance is a characteristic visual identifier of pigment-dyed corduroy.

7.	Fabric Weight and Drape: The weight of corduroy varies depending on the wale count and fiber content. Wide wale corduroy tends to be heavier and has less drape, while pinwale corduroy is lighter and drapes more easily

## Denim

Our literature review helped uncover the following key visual identifiers for denim fabrics:

1. **Diagonal Twill Weave**: _Denim is characterized by a distinctive diagonal ribbing pattern created by its twill weave construction_. In this weave, the weft (horizontal) thread passes under two or more warp (vertical) threads, creating the diagonal lines visible on the fabric's surface. This twill weave is what sets denim apart from other cotton fabrics like cotton duck.

2. Predominantly Indigo Blue Color: The most common denim is indigo denim, where the warp threads are dyed indigo blue, while the weft threads remain white. This specific dyeing and weaving process results in a fabric that is predominantly blue on one side and mostly white on the other side. While denim is available in other colors, the indigo blue shade is its most recognizable visual identifier.

3. Selvedge Edge (in Some Cases): Denim woven on traditional shuttle looms often features a selvedge edge, which is a self-finished edge that prevents the fabric from unraveling. The selvedge is typically identified by a band of contrasting colored threads (often red or white) running along the edge of the fabric. While not all denim has a selvedge edge,
   its presence is a visual indicator of denim woven on a shuttle loom, which is often associated with higher quality and craftsmanship.

4. Weight and Texture: Denim comes in various weights and textures. The sources note that a yard of denim fabric can weigh anywhere from 9 to 32 ounces. Heavier denim tends to be more durable and has a stiffer drape, while lighter denim is more suitable for shirts and dresses. The texture of denim can range from smooth to slightly hairy depending on the type of cotton fibers and finishing processes used.

5. Fading and Wear Patterns: Denim is known for its ability to develop unique fading and wear patterns over time. This is especially true for raw denim, which is unwashed and untreated, allowing it to conform to the wearer's body and develop personalized fading patterns and whiskering. The way denim fades and wears is considered part of its visual appeal and contributes to its overall aesthetic.


‌The diagonal twill weave is a characterisitic feature of denim. The various types of twill weave patterns seen in Denim fabric are:
1. In right-hand twill (RHT), the diagonal lines run from bottom left to top right.
- RHT is the most common twill pattern used in denim production.
- RHT is sometimes called Z-twill because the lines look like the letter "Z."
- RHT creates a flatter, smoother surface.
- Levi's is known for using right-hand twill denim.

2. In left-hand twill (LHT), the diagonal lines run from bottom right to top left.
- LHT is sometimes called S-twill because the lines look like the letter "S."
- LHT denim tends to be softer than RHT denim after wearing and washing.
- LHT denim creates more vertical fades than RHT denim.
- Lee is known for using left-hand twill denim.

3. Broken twill (BT) alternates the direction of the diagonal lines, creating a zig-zag pattern.
- Wrangler invented broken twill denim in 1964 to prevent leg twisting.
- The zig-zag pattern in broken twill is most visible on the inside of the jeans.
- The twill weave in denim fabric is usually a 3x1 twill, which means that three warp yarns are used for each weft yarn.
- The warp yarns are usually dyed indigo (blue), and the weft yarn is usually left white (ecru).
- Leaving the weft yarn undyed saves money on production because the weft is not visible on the outside of the fabric.

## Linen

1. **Linen has a distinctive texture that is slightly slubby and irregular.**
- This is due to the presence of small, soft, irregular lumps called slubs that occur randomly along the length of the linen yarn.
- In the past, slubs were considered defects, but today they are often seen as part of the aesthetic appeal of linen.
- The very finest linen has no slubs at all.

2. **Linen fabric has a natural luster that is slightly glossy.**

3. Linen wrinkles easily.
- This is because linen has poor elasticity.
- The tendency to wrinkle is often considered part of linen's charm.
- Some linen garments are designed to be air-dried and worn without ironing.

4 .Linen fibers have nodes, which add to the flexibility and texture of the fabric.
- **The cross-section of the linen fiber is made up of irregular polygonal shapes, which also contribute to the coarse texture of the fabric.**

5. Linen fabric feels cool to the touch.
- This is because linen has higher conductivity than other fabrics.

6. Linen is typically a more expensive textile produced in relatively small quantities than other fabrics, like cotton.
- This is due to the difficulty of working with linen thread and the fact that the flax plant requires a great deal of attention.
- Flax thread is not elastic, which makes it difficult to weave without breaking threads.

## Wool

1. Wool fabric has a natural waviness called “crimp.”
- Crimp contributes to wool's insulative properties by trapping air.
- The amount of crimp varies depending on the type of wool.
- Merino wool, for example, is known for being very fine with a lot of crimp.
- Other types of wool, like mohair, also have a high crimp.

2. Wool can be felted.
- Felting happens when wool fibers are agitated, causing the microscopic barbs on the surface of the fibers to hook together.
- Felting can be done intentionally to create certain fabrics, or it can happen unintentionally, for example, on a sheep's back.

3. Wool is naturally flame resistant.

4. Wool is typically a creamy white color, but some breeds of sheep produce wool in other natural colors, like black, brown, and silver.

5. Wool can be made into a variety of fabrics, including:
- Tweed
- Felt
- Worsted
- Melton

6. Wool fibers have scales on their surface, which can be seen under a microscope. These scales overlap and point towards the tip of the fiber.
- The scales and the crimp of the wool fiber help it to bind into yarn.
- Wool readily absorbs moisture but the fibers themselves are not hollow. Wool can absorb almost one-third of its weight in water.

7. The quality of wool is determined by the fineness of the fibers, which is measured in microns.
- Fine wool, such as Merino, has a smaller diameter and can have up to 40 crimps per centimeter.
- Coarser wools, like Karakul, may have fewer than one crimp per centimeter.

8. Wool can be dyed in a wide variety of colors. However, wool is generally a creamy white color. Some breeds of sheep produce wool in natural colors, such as black, brown, silver, and random mixes.
- Wool fabrics can have a variety of different textures, depending on the type of wool used and the way it is processed.
- For example, worsted wool is a smooth, hard-surfaced yarn, while woolen yarn is softer and typically used for knitting.
